# 8.4 Manage Supplier Risk

## Learning Objectives

- Define third-party/supplier risk management (TPRM)
- Identify essential security clauses in vendor contracts
- Explain the "Right to Audit" concept
- Describe the process of establishing Service Level Agreements (SLAs) for security

---

## The Scope of Supplier Risk

Every vendor your organization uses expands your Attack Surface. 

- If you hire an offshore development team to write an application, their security posture becomes your security posture. 
- If you use a SaaS provider for HR, a breach of their databases is legally a breach of your employee data.

**Regulatory Impact**: Under GDPR, HIPAA, and CCPA, the organization that collects the data (the Controller) is legally responsible for safeguarding it, even if they outsource the storage or processing to a third party (the Processor). **You can outsource the work; you cannot outsource the legal liability.**

---

## Third-Party Risk Assessments

Before signing a contract and sending data to a supplier, a formal security assessment must be conducted.

1. **Information Gathering**: The vendor completes a standardized security questionnaire (e.g., SIG Core, CSA CAIQ) detailing their encryption standards, access controls, and incident response plans.
2. **Evidence Review**: The organization requests proof (e.g., reviewing their SOC 2 Type II report, penetration testing summaries, or ISO 27001 certificate).
3. **Risk Scoring**: The vendor is assigned a risk score (High, Medium, Low) based on the sensitivity of the data they will handle and the strength of their controls.
4. **Risk Acceptance**: Management must formally accept the residual risk of doing business with the vendor before the contract is signed.

---

## Security Contract Clauses

The contract is the only leverage you have over a third party. If a security requirement is not explicitly in the contract, the vendor is not obligated to perform it.

### Essential Security Clauses
1. **Right to Audit**: A critical clause granting your organization (or a designated independent auditor) the legal right to inspect the vendor's facilities, systems, and records to verify compliance with security standards. Without this, you have no way to verify they are keeping your data safe.
2. **Data Ownership and Return**: Explicitly states that your organization retains ownership of all data, and mandates secure return or destruction (crypto-shredding) of the data upon contract termination.
3. **Breach Notification Timeline**: Mandates that the vendor must notify your organization within a specific, aggressive timeframe (e.g., "within 24 hours of discovery") if they suspect a security incident affecting your data. Not "when convenient."
4. **Security SLAs (Service Level Agreements)**: Contractually binding metrics. For example:
    - *Patching SLA*: Vendor must apply critical CVE patches within 7 days.
    - *Availability SLA*: Vendor guarantees 99.99% uptime.
    - Included in the contract are **financial penalties** if the vendor fails to meet these SLAs.
5. **Flow-Down Clauses**: Requires the vendor to enforce these exact same security requirements down to their own sub-contractors (the "fourth parties").

---

## Ongoing Monitoring

Third-party risk management is an ongoing lifecycle, not a one-time checklist at procurement.

- **Annual Re-assessments**: Vendors processing critical data must be reassessed annually (reviewing their latest SOC 2 report, pentest results).
- **Continuous Monitoring**: Using OSINT (Open Source Intelligence) or commercial tools (like SecurityScorecard or BitSight) to continuously gauge the external security posture of key suppliers.

---

## Exam Focus Points

1. **Liability Cannot be Outsourced**: The data owner is always ultimately accountable to regulators and customers, regardless of which vendor dropped the ball.
2. **Right to Audit**: The most heavily tested concept in supplier risk. It is the legal mechanism allowing verification of vendor claims.
3. **Contractual Leverage**: SLAs, breach notification timelines, and data destruction mandates must be explicitly written into the contract *before* signing.
4. **Third vs. Fourth Party Risk**: The supply chain goes deep. Your vendor's sub-contractor (fourth party) is a risk to you. Flow-down clauses manage this.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **TPRM** | Third-Party Risk Management |
| **SLA** | Service Level Agreement; contractually binding performance and security metrics |
| **Right to Audit** | Contract clause allowing a client to inspect a vendor's security controls |
| **SOC 2 Type II** | An independent audit report validating a service organization's security controls |
| **Flow-Down Clause** | Contract provision forcing a vendor to apply the same security rules to their subcontractors |
