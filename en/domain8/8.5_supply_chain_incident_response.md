# 8.5 Supply Chain Incident Response

## Learning Objectives

- Integrate third-party breaches into the Incident Response lifecycle
- Explain the role of the Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) during a crisis
- Formulate a coordinated response plan with vendors
- Define mitigation strategies during active supply chain attacks

---

## The Supply Chain Threat Scenario

A normal incident (Domain 7) involves your servers being attacked. A **supply chain incident** occurs when a critical vendor is compromised, or a vulnerability is publicly disclosed in a third-party open-source library that your software relies on.

**The Challenge**: You do not control the vendor's network, nor can you instantly rewrite an open-source library. You are dependent on others to resolve the root cause, but you are responsible to your customers to mitigate the bleeding immediately.

---

## Incident Response: Supply Chain Integration

Your standard Incident Response Plan (PICERL) must account for third parties.

### 1. Preparation
The foundation of supply chain IR relies entirely on **Asset Inventory**.
- You cannot respond to an alert about a compromised library (e.g., Log4Shell) if you do not know which of your 50 applications use that library.
- **The SBOM (Software Bill of Materials)** is the silver bullet here. A centralized SBOM repository allows the IR team to search for the vulnerable component across the entire enterprise in seconds.
- Maintain an updated, 24/7 **emergency contact list for all critical vendors**.

### 2. Identification
How do you know the supply chain is compromised?
- **Vendor Notification**: The vendor invokes the "Breach Notification Clause" in your contract and calls you.
- **Threat Intelligence**: A zero-day is announced on security feeds regarding an open-source library you use.
- **Internal Anomaly**: Your Threat Hunters catch the third-party network monitoring tool Beaconing strange traffic to a Russian IP address (e.g., the SolarWinds scenario).

### 3. Containment
Because you cannot patch a vendor's code or an external SaaS provider instantly, containment focuses on **Isolation**.
- **Virtual Patching**: Deploying WAF/IPS rules to block the newly discovered exploit patterns targeting the vulnerable library.
- **Network Segmentation**: If a vendor's software (like an IT management agent) is believed to be compromised, aggressively firewall it off. Cut its internet access. Allow it only the bare minimum internal access to function, or shut it down entirely (if the business can tolerate the outage).
- **Federation Severance**: If a SaaS provider is breached, immediately sever the SSO (SAML/OIDC) trust in your Identity Provider (e.g., Azure AD/Okta) to prevent attackers from pivoting from the compromised SaaS app back into your corporate network.

### 4. Eradication and Recovery
- Wait for the vendor to release the official patch or the open-source community to update the library.
- Once available, follow the **Emergency Patching Process** (Domain 7) to push the fix to production rapidly.
- Validate the fix using active scanning.

## Coordinated Response and Communication

When a major supply chain attack occurs, your customers will demand answers.

- **The "Are we affected?" Drill**: When a massive vulnerability hits the news, your customers' CISOs will email your CISO asking if your software uses the vulnerable component.
- **Proactive Communication**: Armed with your internal SBOMs, your IR team can quickly verify if your software is compromised. You must legally and ethically communicate the risk to your customers. Evasive answers destroy trust.

---

## The "Shift-Left" Feedback Loop

At the "Lessons Learned" phase of a supply chain incident, the feedback loop usually dictates changes to earlier SDLC domains:
- If a downloaded library contained malware, the organization must implement **SCA tools** and an internal proxy repository (Domain 5).
- If a vendor breached their SLA and hid the breach for three weeks, Procurement must invoke penalty clauses and potentially terminate the relationship (Domain 8.4).

---

## Exam Focus Points

1. **SBOM = Speed**: The Software Bill of Materials is mission-critical in the identification phase of a supply chain incident. Without it, finding the vulnerability takes weeks.
2. **Containment by Isolation**: If a third-party tool is compromised, the primary containment strategy is network isolation and severing trust boundaries (SSO, API keys).
3. **Communication**: Joint response requires clear, proactive communication with customers regarding the blast radius of a third-party failure.
4. **Virtual Patching**: The primary mitigation while waiting for an upstream open-source or commercial vendor to write the actual patch.

---

## Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **SBOM** | Software Bill of Materials; an exhaustive list of components enabling rapid vulnerability location |
| **Virtual Patching** | Using network controls (WAF/IPS) to block exploits against vulnerable third-party code |
| **Federation Severance** | Breaking the SSO trust relationship with a compromised vendor to prevent lateral movement |
| **Asset Inventory** | A comprehensive list of all hardware, software, and vendor relationships in the organization |
